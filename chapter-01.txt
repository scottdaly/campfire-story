CHAPTER ONE — Put-In

The water at the put-in was the color of strong tea, and Biscuit
was in it before the first canoe touched the surface.

They loaded the boats the way they always had: Marcus in the stern
because he claimed he could read water, Dee in the bow because she
actually could, and Sam wedged amid the packs with the map he had
laminated for the occasion. Biscuit rode wherever Biscuit wanted.

Marcus had organized the trip the way he organized everything —
loudly, and three weeks too late. He was the one who'd found the
photograph in a shoebox of his late father's things and decided,
with the certainty he applied to all his worst ideas, that the
three of them had been there once, together, as kids. Nobody
remembered the day. That was the point, he said, though he could
never explain what the point actually was.

Dee said little and packed twice. She had grown up on water like
this, in a town that no longer had a name on the new maps, and she
trusted a paddle the way other people trusted door locks. When
Marcus declared their heading she would wait a beat, study the far
shore, and adjust it by a few degrees without comment. He never
noticed. She preferred it that way.

Sam noticed everything and trusted nothing he hadn't laminated.
He had brought a spare compass, a spare lighter, and a spare pair
of glasses he didn't tell anyone about, because the last time he'd
gone into the bush unprepared he'd been nineteen and it had not
gone well. He didn't talk about that either. He talked, instead,
about contour lines, at length, until Dee threatened to feed the
map to the dog.

By late afternoon they found the campsite from the photograph, or at least a place
that seemed close enough. Granite shelf, leaning jack pine, a fire ring someone
had built and someone else had improved. They pitched the tents,
got a fire going, and watched the clouds stack up dark across the
lake while the chili heated through.

"It'll pass north of us," Marcus said, in the voice of a man who
could read water.

Dee looked at the clouds, then at the bow of the canoe, then at
nothing in particular. "Stake the flies down," she said. "Both of
them. Now." Sam was already doing it. He had read the forecast in
three different apps before they lost signal and had believed the
worst of them, on principle.

It did not pass north of them.

The storm came in low and fast, flattening the tent flies and
turning the fire ring into a birdbath. They dove for the tents
with the chili half-eaten — all except Marcus, who stood a moment
longer in the rain with his father's certainty draining out of him,
until Dee reached out of the tent and hauled him in by the collar.
Through the nylon they listened to the rain drive sideways until
the fire gave one long hiss and went out, and the dark after that
was total.
